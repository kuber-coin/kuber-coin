## 1. Introduction

This document establishes the legal and operational boundaries for the KuberCoin blockchain network. It clarifies responsibilities, disclaimers, and jurisdictional considerations for node operators, developers, integrators, and users.

## 2. Network Governance Model

### 2.1 Decentralized Network

KuberCoin is a **decentralized, permissionless blockchain network**. There is no central authority, company, or foundation that controls the network.

**Key Principles:**
- **No Central Entity:** No single entity owns or operates the KuberCoin network
- **Open Source:** All software is open source under MIT License
- **Consensus-Driven:** Protocol changes require network consensus
- **Voluntary Participation:** Node operators choose to participate voluntarily

### 2.2 Reference Implementation

The KuberCoin Node software is a **reference implementation** of the protocol. Other compatible implementations may exist or be developed.

## 5. Operator Liability Assumptions

### 5.1 Node Operators

**Responsible For:**
- **Security:** Securing their own systems and private keys
- **Compliance:** Following local laws and regulations
- **Content:** Transactions they create or relay
- **Resources:** Bandwidth, storage, and computational costs
- **Privacy:** Protecting their own operational data

**Not Responsible For:**
- Network-wide consensus failures
- Other operators' violations
- Protocol-level vulnerabilities (unless known and not disclosed responsibly)
- Third-party integrations or services

### 5.2 Miners

**Additional Considerations:**
- Must not engage in selfish mining or 51% attacks
- Must not censor valid transactions arbitrarily
- May face energy consumption regulations
- May face cryptocurrency mining restrictions

### 5.3 Exchange Operators

**Must:**
- Register as Money Service Business (MSB) if required
- Implement KYC/AML procedures as required by law
- Comply with securities regulations if applicable
- Secure customer funds appropriately
- Maintain transparent fee structures

### 5.4 Wallet Providers

**Must:**
- Secure private keys appropriately (or enable users to do so)
- Disclose custody arrangements (custodial vs non-custodial)
- Implement security best practices
- Provide clear terms of service
- Comply with local financial regulations

## 6. Jurisdictional Risk Notes

### 6.1 Regulatory Landscape

**As of March 2026, cryptocurrency regulation varies widely:**

**Permissive Jurisdictions:**
- Switzerland, Singapore, Malta, Estonia, UAE
- Generally favorable regulatory frameworks
- Clear licensing requirements

**Restrictive Jurisdictions:**
- China, Algeria, Bangladesh, Bolivia, Nepal
- Cryptocurrency transactions may be prohibited
- Operating nodes or mining may be illegal

**Unclear/Evolving:**
- United States (state-by-state variance)
- European Union (MiCA regulations phasing in)
- India, Nigeria, Russia (frequent policy changes)

### 6.2 Jurisdictional Risks

**Node Operators May Face:**
- **Licensing Requirements:** Some jurisdictions require MSB/financial licenses
- **Tax Obligations:** Income tax on mining rewards, capital gains on transactions
- **Data Regulations:** GDPR, data localization requirements
- **Sanctions Compliance:** OFAC, UN sanctions on certain addresses
- **Criminal Liability:** If used for money laundering, terrorism financing, etc.

**Recommended Actions:**
- **Consult Local Counsel:** Understand your jurisdiction's specific requirements
- **Monitor Developments:** Cryptocurrency regulations change rapidly
- **Implement Compliance:** KYC/AML if operating exchange/wallet service
- **Geographic Blocking:** Consider blocking users from high-risk jurisdictions
- **Transparent Operations:** Maintain clear terms of service and privacy policies

### 6.3 Cross-Border Considerations

**International Operations:**
- Multiple jurisdictions may claim authority
- Conflicts of law may arise
- Enforcement varies by country
- Extradition treaties may apply

**Best Practices:**
- Establish clear terms of service specifying governing law
- Implement geographic restrictions if necessary
- Maintain legal counsel in primary operating jurisdictions
- Consider structuring entities in favorable jurisdictions
